The fastest way to delay a shipment is a documentation error, and the second fastest is a missing document. Goods rarely sit in port because of what they are; they sit because of what is wrong with the paperwork describing them. A disciplined documentation process is the cheapest insurance in trade.

This is a working checklist, not legal advice. Requirements vary by commodity, origin, and destination, so treat it as a starting framework rather than a complete answer.

## Core commercial documents

These travel with nearly every international shipment and must agree with one another exactly:

- Commercial invoice with accurate values, terms, and descriptions.
- Packing list reconciled to the invoice line by line.
- Bill of lading or air waybill matching the consignment.
- Certificate of origin, attested where required.

## Regulatory and conformity documents

Many goods require additional clearance depending on type and destination:

- Certificates of conformity or quality for regulated products.
- Inspection certificates where pre-shipment inspection applies.
- Permits or licenses for restricted or dual-use items.
- Health, phytosanitary, or hazardous-material certificates as relevant.

## The discipline that prevents delays

The documents matter less than the consistency between them. Customs systems compare figures automatically, and a single mismatch between invoice and packing list can trigger a manual hold. Reconcile everything before the goods ship, not after they land.
