A transformer travels thousands of kilometers by sea without incident, then nearly derails a project in its final kilometers to site. The last mile of mega-infrastructure logistics is where ocean-scale planning meets ground-level reality, and it is frequently the riskiest leg of the entire journey.

Oversized and overweight cargo cannot simply be trucked. It requires route surveys, permits, escorts, and sometimes temporary modification of the infrastructure it passes. None of this happens on short notice.

## The constraints multiply on the ground

At sea, the cargo is one of many containers. On a regional road, a single transformer or turbine section can exceed bridge ratings, overhead clearances, and turning radii. Each constraint must be identified, measured, and engineered around before the move begins.

- Survey the full route for height, weight, and width limits.
- Secure permits and police escorts ahead of the movement window.
- Plan for night moves or temporary closures where required.

## Timing is the hidden variable

Heavy-haul movements are often restricted to specific hours and seasons. A delayed customs release can cause a missed permit window, which cascades into weeks of delay. Synchronizing clearance, transport, and site readiness is the core discipline.

## Site readiness closes the loop

The last mile does not end at the gate. The receiving site must have the crane capacity, foundation, and access ready when the cargo arrives. Delivering a turbine to a site that cannot lift it is a failure of coordination, not transport.
